## 1. Classic Advertising Frameworks

### David Ogilvy's Principles

Ogilvy's fundamental belief: **advertising must sell**. Everything else is secondary.

Key principles:
- **Research first**: "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals."
- **Brand image is 95% of what you sell**: Every ad is a long-term investment in the brand's personality.
- **Facts sell**: "The more facts you tell, the more you sell." Be specific with concrete numbers and precise details.
- **Headlines do 80% of the work**: Five times as many people read headlines as body copy.
- **The consumer is not a moron**: "She is your wife. Don't insult her intelligence."
- **Big ideas**: "Unless your campaign contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night."
- **Persuasion over creativity**: "I don't want you to tell me you find it creative. I want you to find it so persuasive that you buy the product."

### Bill Bernbach's 10 Principles (The Creative Revolution)

Bernbach may have been the single most important figure in 20th-century advertising. He placed creativity before research and believed advertising must respect public intelligence.

1. **Product Essence**: "Go to the essence of the product. State the product's essence in the simplest terms of its basic advantage."
2. **Active Product Role**: Make the product a participant in scenes, not background decoration.
3. **Integrated Art and Copy**: Design and writing must be conceived together from the start as unified elements.
4. **Vitality and Personality**: Advertising needs energy and distinctive character to differentiate and persuade.
5. **Meaningful Gimmicks**: Avoid superficial tricks unless the trick itself communicates something about the product.
6. **Truth**: Honesty builds trust and drives sales. It is both strategically smart and ethically sound.
7. **Relevance**: A wonderfully creative execution gets a big "So what" if it isn't meaningful. Relevance trumps excitement.
8. **Simplicity**: Focus on single ideas, not complexity. People have limited attention spans.
9. **Courage**: "Safe ideas can kill you." If it's been done before, competition is ready for it. Your only chance is advertising they've never seen before.
10. **Visibility**: Without attention, all other efforts fail. You must stand out to register.

**Bernbach's defining quote**: "Be sure your advertising is saying something that will inform and serve the consumer, and saying it like it's never been said before."

### The Creative Brief Framework

The standard agency creative brief distills strategy into a single-page document with:

- **The Problem**: What business/brand challenge are we solving?
- **Target Audience**: Who are we talking to? (demographics + psychographics)
- **Key Insight**: The human truth or tension the campaign will address
- **Single-Minded Proposition (SMP)**: One clear thing we want the audience to take away
- **Support/Reasons to Believe**: Evidence backing the proposition
- **Tone of Voice**: How the brand should feel
- **Mandatories**: Non-negotiable brand elements
- **Desired Response**: What we want people to think, feel, or do

**Critical rule**: Everything fits on one page. If the brief isn't simple, the work won't be either.

## 2. The SUCCESS Model - Made to Stick

Chip & Dan Heath's research into why some ideas survive and others die, codified into six principles (SUCCESs):

### S - Simple
**Find the core and share it compactly.**

- Strip away everything non-essential until only the core message remains.
- Not about "dumbing down" -- it's about prioritizing ruthlessly.
- Example: Southwest Airlines' entire strategy compressed to "THE Low Fare Airline." Every employee decision (Should we add meals? No -- that's not low-fare) flows from this core.
- **Commander's Intent** in the military: a single clear objective that survives contact with chaos.
- The "inverted pyramid" of journalism: lead with the most important thing.

**Application to campaigns**: The tagline or hook must encode the single most important thing. If it requires explanation, it's not simple enough.

### U - Unexpected
**Violate expectations to get attention, then satisfy curiosity to hold it.**

- Surprise gets attention. Interest keeps attention.
- Create a "knowledge gap" -- open curiosity before closing it.
- The best unexpected elements are "postdictable" -- surprising in the moment, obvious in hindsight.
- Example: Enclave minivan ad that begins as a serene family drive, then shocks with a car crash -- making the safety message unforgettable.

**Two-part mechanism**:
1. Break a pattern to create surprise (attention)
2. Create a mystery or knowledge gap (sustained interest)

### C - Concrete
**Make abstract ideas tangible and sensory.**

- Use specific, vivid images rather than abstract concepts.
- "A bird in the hand" beats "a sure thing."
- Example: Subway's "Jared Diet" -- an abstract claim about healthy food became concrete through one man's documented weight loss journey.
- Concrete details make ideas memorable and actionable.

**Application**: Don't say "high quality." Show a specific scene, detail, or demonstration that proves quality.

### C - Credible
**Give people reasons to believe.**

- External credibility: experts, celebrities, data.
- Internal credibility: vivid details, specific numbers, the "Sinatra Test" ("If I can make it there, I'll make it anywhere" -- one example proves the point).
- Anti-authority credibility: real people with real experience (more powerful than celebrities for authenticity).
- The "testable credential": let people verify for themselves ("See the difference" / "Try it free").

**Application**: The best credibility is experiential -- let the audience prove the claim to themselves.

### E - Emotional
**Make people care by appealing to feelings, not just facts.**

- People donate more to one starving child (with name and photo) than to "3 million starving children" (statistics numb).
- Associate ideas with things people already care about (self-interest, identity, values).
- The "power of one" -- individual stories beat statistical arguments.
- Appeal to identity: "What would a person like me do?"

**Key finding**: Analytical thinking actually suppresses emotional response. Once you put on your "analytical hat," giving drops. Lead with emotion, follow with logic.

### S - Stories
**Wrap ideas in narrative to make them actionable and memorable.**

- Stories are "flight simulators" for the brain -- they let people mentally rehearse.
- Three core story plots that work:
  - **The Challenge Plot**: Overcoming obstacles (David vs. Goliath). Inspires action.
  - **The Connection Plot**: Bridging divides (racial, class, social). Inspires togetherness.
  - **The Creativity Plot**: Mental breakthroughs, aha moments. Inspires new thinking.
- Stories provide both simulation (how to act) and inspiration (motivation to act).

**Application**: Every great campaign tells a story, even in 6 seconds. There's a character, a tension, and a resolution.

## 3. Creative Tension & Contrast

### Cognitive Dissonance as a Creative Tool

When ads make viewers question their beliefs, they trigger introspection, making the ad memorable. The psychological clash between beliefs and new information creates motivational tension -- and the brain is driven to resolve it.

**How great campaigns use dissonance**:
- Dove "Real Beauty": Created dissonance between beauty industry standards and women's actual self-image. The tension was between "beauty ads show perfect models" and "only 2% of women consider themselves beautiful."
- Share a Coke: Created dissonance for loyal non-Coke drinkers by associating personal identity (your name!) with Coca-Cola.

### Bisociation: Koestler's Theory of the Creative Act

Arthur Koestler's theory explains the mechanism behind creative breakthroughs:

**Core concept**: Bisociation is perceiving a situation in **two self-consistent but habitually incompatible frames of reference** simultaneously. Unlike ordinary "association" (thinking within one frame), bisociation connects two entirely separate frames to create something new.

**Three domains of bisociation**:
- **Humor**: The punchline connects two frames (setup creates one expectation, punchline delivers another)
- **Discovery**: Scientific insight connects two previously unrelated fields
- **Art**: Metaphor, analogy, and creative expression bridge incompatible frames

**Application to advertising**: The best creative ideas bisociate the product truth with a human truth from an entirely different domain. "Think Different" bisociates a computer brand with revolutionary historical figures. Old Spice bisociates men's body wash with absurdist action-hero comedy.

### The Contrast Principle

The human tendency to perceive and evaluate stimuli relative to other stimuli, not in absolute terms.

**Application techniques**:
- Before/After comparisons
- Comparison tables (competitor vs. brand)
- Juxtaposition of problem and solution
- Tonal contrast (humor in a serious category, seriousness in a lighthearted category)

**Key insight**: The most memorable campaigns often operate in a tonal register that is unexpected for their category -- a toilet paper brand that's hilarious, a tech brand that's deeply human, a beer brand that's philosophical.

## 4. Emotional Resonance

### The Data on Emotion vs. Reason

- **31% of emotional ads succeeded** vs. only **16% of rational ads** (IPA dataBANK analysis).
- Emotional campaigns produce roughly **twice the profit** of rational campaigns.
- 90% of consumers are more likely to recall ads that use humor.
- Emotional triggers (humor, awe, fear, joy, sadness) significantly influence sharing propensity.

### The Core Emotional Triggers

**1. Humor**
- Reduces perceived risk, creates positive brand associations.
- Excels in shareability and recall.
- Works best when the humor is inherent to the product truth (not bolted on).
- Risk: humor can overwhelm the message if not tethered to the brand.

**2. Fear/Anxiety**
- Effective for public service advertising and insurance/safety products.
- Must be paired with clear action steps (fear without resolution creates paralysis, not action).
- Fear is more effective for cross-platform sharing of video ads.

**3. Belonging/Community**
- Connects the brand with personal or social identity.
- Tribe-building: "People like us do things like this" (Seth Godin).
- Example: Apple's "Think Different" -- buying Apple means joining the tribe of creative misfits.

**4. Aspiration/Inspiration**
- Joy and happiness appeals link positive emotions to product use.
- The "Challenge Plot" story type inspires action through overcoming obstacles.
- Example: Nike's entire brand promise is aspirational activation.

**5. Nostalgia**
- Triggers warm, bittersweet emotions that increase willingness to spend.
- Creates sense of continuity and trust.
- Example: Coca-Cola's persistent use of heritage and tradition imagery.

**6. Surprise/Awe**
- The most powerful driver of viral sharing.
- Creates "flashbulb memory" -- the brain encodes surprising events more deeply.
- Must be relevant surprise, not random shock.

### Platform-Specific Emotional Effects

- Humor enhances sharing intention for in-platform video ads.
- Fear is more effective for cross-platform sharing.
- Awe and inspiration drive the highest organic reach.
- Nostalgia performs exceptionally well on Facebook (older demographic).

## 5. Famous Campaign Breakdowns

### "Think Different" -- Apple (1997)
**Agency**: TBWA\Chiat\Day

**Context**: Apple was near bankruptcy. Brand image was so tarnished they couldn't sell on product features -- nobody would invest in a dying company's products.

**The Insight**: People who change the world think differently. Apple makes tools for those people.

**The Creative Leap**: Don't talk about computers at all. Celebrate the creative misfits who changed the world and associate Apple with that spirit.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Simple**: Two words. An identity statement, not a product claim.
- **Unexpected**: A computer company that doesn't talk about computers.
- **Emotional**: Deep aspiration -- "I want to be one of those people."
- **Story**: Every person featured (Einstein, Gandhi, Lennon, etc.) embodied a challenge plot.
- **Credible**: These weren't paid endorsers -- they were genuine icons of different thinking.

**Result**: Apple's stock tripled within 12 months despite having no significant new products.

**Key lesson**: When your product can't speak for itself, speak for the values your audience holds.

---

### "Just Do It" -- Nike (1988)
**Agency**: Wieden+Kennedy

**Context**: Nike was a struggling niche brand losing ground to Reebok. Needed to expand beyond serious athletes to reach women and casual fitness enthusiasts.

**The Insight**: Everyone has plans they need to move to action. Humans are dreamers looking for motivation.

**The Human Truth**: The universal gap between intention and action. We all know we should exercise. We all find reasons not to.

**The Creative Leap**: Nike isn't selling shoes. Nike is selling the moment of commitment -- the instant you stop thinking and start doing.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Simple**: Three words. A command. A philosophy.
- **Concrete**: It's an action verb -- you know exactly what "doing it" means in your own life.
- **Emotional**: Aspirational without being preachy. It meets you where you are.
- **Universal**: Applies to running, but also to asking for a raise, starting a business, or any act of courage.

**Result**: Sales increased 1,000% over the following decade.

**Key lesson**: The best taglines transcend the product category and become life philosophies.

---

### "Got Milk?" (1993)
**Agency**: Goodby Silverstein & Partners

**The Insight**: Nobody thinks about milk until they don't have it. The deprivation scenario is universally relatable.

**The Creative Leap**: Instead of promoting milk's benefits (boring, expected), dramatize the agony of not having milk when you desperately need it (unexpected, visceral).

**Why it stuck**:
- **Unexpected**: Inverted the typical "drink milk because it's healthy" approach.
- **Concrete**: Specific scenarios (eating peanut butter, eating cookies) that everyone has experienced.
- **Simple**: Two-word question that becomes a cultural catchphrase.
- **Emotional**: The frustration of running out creates genuine empathy.

**Result**: Ran for ~20 years, boosted milk sales by 6%, became one of the most recognized taglines in history.

**Key lesson**: The absence of a product can be more compelling than its presence. Deprivation stories are powerful.

---

### "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like" -- Old Spice (2010)
**Agency**: Wieden+Kennedy

**The Insight**: Women purchase 60%+ of men's body wash. The secondary audience (women) holds the purchasing power.

**The Human Truth**: The gap between fantasy and reality in relationships -- playful, not threatening.

**The Creative Leap**: Speak to women about their men, using absurdist humor and a hyper-confident persona that both genders enjoy.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Unexpected**: A men's body wash ad that speaks to women. Absurdist transitions that defy physics.
- **Simple**: One character, one continuous shot (seemingly), one escalating joke.
- **Emotional**: Humor -- specifically absurdist, confident humor that's inherently shareable.
- **Concrete**: "Look at your man. Now back to me." Specific, vivid, commanding.

**Result**: Transformed Old Spice from an outdated brand into a modern cultural phenomenon. Became one of the most viral ads in history.

**Key lesson**: Identify the real buyer. Use tonal surprise (absurdist humor in a boring category). Make it remixable.

### "Share a Coke" -- Coca-Cola (2011)
**Agency**: Ogilvy Sydney (originated in Australia)

**The Insight**: Mass-produced products feel impersonal. People crave personalization and individual recognition.

**The Creative Leap**: Replace the world's most iconic logo with individual first names. Turn a commodity into a personal artifact.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Concrete**: Your actual name on a bottle. Tangible, specific, personal.
- **Emotional**: Belonging, recognition, the gift of giving someone "their" Coke.
- **Story**: Every bottle becomes a micro-story ("I found my name!" or "I gave one to my friend").
- **Self-spreading**: The product became the media -- people photographed and shared their named bottles, creating millions of pieces of organic content.

**Result**: Reversed a decade of declining Coke consumption. Generated enormous UGC and social media engagement.

**Key lesson**: Let the product become the medium. Give consumers something to do with the brand, not just consume from it.

---

### "Real Beauty" -- Dove (2004)
**Agency**: Ogilvy & Mather

**The Insight**: Only 2% of women worldwide consider themselves beautiful. The beauty industry's standards are actively harmful.

**The Human Truth**: The gap between how beauty is portrayed and how real women see themselves.

**The Creative Leap**: A soap brand takes a stand against the beauty industry itself. Use real women, not models. Challenge the very category you operate in.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Unexpected**: A beauty brand saying beauty standards are wrong. Category disruption.
- **Credible**: Real women. Real bodies. The "anti-authority" credibility of authentic people.
- **Emotional**: Deep, identity-level resonance. Women felt seen for the first time by a brand.
- **Story**: Movement-building -- not a campaign but a cause.
- **Simple**: "Real Beauty" -- two words that reframe everything.

**Result**: Sales grew from $2.5B to $4B over 10 years. Campaign ran for 20+ years, evolving into a full movement.

**Key lesson**: Identify a cultural tension in your category and take a bold stand against the status quo. Align your brand with a cause bigger than your product.

---

### "Priceless" -- MasterCard (1997)
**Agency**: McCann Erickson

**The Insight**: People don't buy things -- they buy experiences and moments.

**The Human Truth**: The most valuable things in life can't be bought, but you need money (and a card) to set them up.

**The Creative Leap**: List mundane purchases with prices, then reveal the priceless emotional payoff they enable.

**Why it stuck**:
- **Simple**: Repeatable format anyone can understand (and parody).
- **Concrete**: Specific prices for specific items.
- **Emotional**: The payoff is always an emotional moment, not a product benefit.
- **Story**: Each ad is a micro-narrative building to an emotional climax.

**Key lesson**: Find the emotional end-benefit behind the functional transaction. The format should be so clear that consumers can create their own versions.

## 6. The Creative Leap

### What Is It?

The creative leap is the mechanism that transforms a **strategic insight** (what to say) into a **creative idea** (how to say it). It is the most mysterious and valuable part of the advertising process.

### Bisociation as the Engine

The creative leap is essentially Koestler's bisociation applied to advertising:

**Frame 1**: The strategic truth (product benefit, human insight, brand positioning)
**Frame 2**: An unrelated cultural, visual, or narrative domain

**The leap**: Connecting these two frames in a way that is surprising yet immediately comprehensible.

**Examples**:
- Nike's insight "overcome your excuses" + the domain of heroic mythology = "Just Do It"
- Dove's insight "beauty standards are harmful" + the domain of social activism = "Real Beauty"
- Old Spice's insight "women buy men's body wash" + the domain of absurdist fantasy = "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like"

### How Top Agencies Approach It

**The general process at agencies like W+K, BBDO, Droga5**:

1. **Immersion**: Deep dive into consumer research, cultural trends, competitive landscape.
2. **Insight Mining**: Using 7+ proven techniques to find the "hiding in plain sight" truth.
3. **Brief Writing**: Compressing everything into a single-page brief with one single-minded proposition.
4. **Ideation**: Generating ideas using multiple methods:
   - **Structural methods** (SIT templates, SCAMPER)
   - **Associative methods** (bisociation, metaphor, analogy)
   - **Disruptive methods** (inversion, absurd alternative, extreme consequence)
5. **Evaluation**: Testing ideas against the brief and against memorability/stickiness criteria.
6. **Refinement**: Iterating on the strongest ideas until execution matches ambition.

### Goldenberg's Eight Creative Templates

Research by Jacob Goldenberg analyzed 200 award-winning ads and found that **89% could be explained by just six creativity templates**. Further: **50% of award winners used these templates vs. only 2.5% of non-winners**.

The eight patterns:

1. **Unification**: An existing element in the ad's environment takes on an additional role. (A billboard's shadow becomes part of the message.)
2. **Activation**: The ad requires viewer participation or interaction.
3. **Metaphor**: A symbolic representation replaces literal communication.
4. **Subtraction**: A key element is removed, and its absence tells the story.
5. **Extreme Consequence**: Exaggerating the result of using (or not using) the product.
6. **Absurd Alternative**: Showing a ridiculous alternative to the product.
7. **Inversion**: Reversing the roles or perspectives (showing the product's absence or competitor's world).
8. **Extreme Effort**: Showing extreme lengths someone goes to for the product, proving its desirability.

**Critical finding**: Because templates are systematic, they can be codified into an algorithm that generates creative ideas programmatically. The template approach is trainable and measurably improves creative output.

## 7. Pattern Interruption

### The Cognitive Science

When the brain encounters a stimulus that **defies its prediction**, it momentarily halts other processes to evaluate the new information. This is involuntary -- you cannot choose not to notice.

**Key mechanisms**:
- **Pre-attentive processing**: Occurs within 200-500 milliseconds. The brain detects anomalies before conscious awareness.
- **Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)**: When multiple similar stimuli are presented, the one that differs is remembered best. Distinctiveness = memorability.
- **Orienting Response**: A neurological reaction to novel stimuli -- pupils dilate, heart rate changes, attention focuses.

### Attention Timeline
- **0-1 second**: Pattern break is detected (pre-attentive)
- **1-2.5 seconds**: Conscious attention engages
- **2.5+ seconds**: Memory encoding begins

**Critical insight**: You can capture attention in 1 second, but you need 2.5+ seconds to embed memory structures. Breaking the pattern is necessary but insufficient -- you must then reward the attention.

### Pattern Interruption Techniques for Campaigns

1. **Visual differentiation**: Look nothing like your category (Magic Spoon cereal's psychedelic packaging).
2. **Tonal subversion**: Be funny in a serious category, or serious in a funny one.
3. **Format breaking**: Coinbase's Super Bowl QR code (no celebrities, no story, just a bouncing QR code). Result: 445,000 sign-ups, crashed servers.
4. **Competitive disruption**: Burger King's "Whopper Detour" geo-fenced McDonald's locations. Result: 37:1 ROAS, 1.5M app downloads in 48 hours.
5. **Experiential surprise**: Dollar Beard Club's absurdist video (under $1,500 production cost, 2M views, 15K subscribers).

### The Von Restorff Effect in Practice

- Items that are **isolated** (different in size, color, shape, or meaning) from their surroundings are remembered 2-3x better.
- This explains why the single bold claim in a sea of feature lists gets remembered.
- This explains why the one brand that looks/sounds nothing like its competitors wins recall.

**Three theories for why it works**:
1. **Gestalt**: Similar items aggregate; isolated items pop out.
2. **Interference reduction**: Isolation reduces confusion with similar stimuli.
3. **Attention boost**: Isolated items involuntarily receive more processing time.

## 8. Simplicity & Memorability

### The Linguistics of Sticky Slogans

Research findings on what makes taglines memorable:

- **Optimal length**: Most liked slogans average **4.9 words**. Most recalled slogans average **3.9 words**.
- **Working memory constraint**: Human working memory processes ~7 (plus/minus 2) chunks. Shorter phrases encode more reliably.
- **Processing fluency**: Simple, common words are liked more (positive attitude). But slightly unusual words increase memory retention (effort = encoding).

**The fluency trade-off**: Easy words = liked more. Hard words = remembered more. The best slogans use easy words in unexpected combinations.
- "Just Do It" -- three of the simplest words in English, combined into a commanding philosophy.
- "Think Different" -- two common words, grammatically unusual, instantly memorable.

### Linguistic Devices That Boost Memorability

1. **Rhyme**: "See the USA in your Chevrolet." Rhyming increases processing fluency and perceived truthfulness (the "rhyme-as-reason" effect).
2. **Alliteration**: "PayPal," "Coca-Cola," "Best Buy." Repeated sounds create phonetic hooks.
3. **Plosive consonants**: Hard sounds (B, D, K, P, T) are more attention-grabbing and memorable than soft sounds.
4. **Rhythm/Cadence**: Slogans with natural rhythmic stress patterns (iambic, trochaic) are easier to remember.
5. **Parallelism**: "Easy to use. Hard to put down." Structural repetition aids encoding.
6. **Antithesis**: "Think small." (VW) Opposing expectations in minimal words.

### Priming Effect

Slogans work through **subconscious priming** -- they influence thoughts and actions without conscious awareness. Exposure to a slogan activates associated brand memories automatically. This is why consistency and repetition matter: each exposure deepens the neural pathway.

### The "Commander's Intent" Principle

From military strategy, adopted by Heath brothers: compress the entire strategy into a single, actionable sentence that survives contact with chaos. If soldiers (or consumers) forget everything else, they remember this.

**Application**: A campaign line must function as Commander's Intent -- it should encode the entire strategy in a way that anyone on the team (or any consumer) can act on without further explanation.

## 9. Human Truth & Insight

### The Hierarchy of Truths

Effective campaigns can draw from three levels, but the deepest level produces the most enduring work:

**Level 1 -- Category Truth**: An observation about the product category.
- "Coffee gives you energy." (True but not distinctive.)
- Shelf life: months. Any competitor can claim it.

**Level 2 -- Brand Truth**: Something specific to the brand's heritage, process, or positioning.
- "We've been brewing since 1850." (Distinctive but not emotional.)
- Shelf life: years. But can feel self-referential.

**Level 3 -- Human Truth**: A fundamental insight into human behavior, emotion, or motivation that is universal, timeless, and often unspoken.
- "The gap between who we are and who we want to be is where motivation lives." (Nike's territory.)
- Shelf life: decades. Transcends demographics and cultures.

### Characteristics of a Great Human Truth

- **Universal but not obvious**: "You do that too? I thought it was just me."
- **Often unspoken**: Lives in the messiness of emotion, motivation, and contradiction.
- **Cannot be manufactured**: Only discovered through genuine empathy and research.
- **Sparks original ideas**: Provides a springboard that enables braver creative work.
- **Emotionally resonant**: When framed correctly, becomes deeply relatable.

### Campaign Examples Mapped to Human Truths

| Campaign | Human Truth | Why It's Universal |
|---|---|---|
| Nike "Just Do It" | The gap between intention and action | Everyone procrastinates. Everyone needs motivation. |
| Dove "Real Beauty" | Only 2% of women self-identify as beautiful | Beauty insecurity is nearly universal among women. |
| MasterCard "Priceless" | The best moments can't be bought | Everyone values experiences over things. |
| Apple "Think Different" | Misfits and rebels change the world | Everyone feels like an outsider sometimes. |
| Heineken 0.0 | Non-drinkers feel excluded from toasting rituals | Social belonging is a fundamental human need. |
| Duolingo "Tattoo DuoOver" | People get embarrassing foreign-language tattoos | Language mistakes are universally cringe-worthy. |
| Goodfood (raccoon campaign) | We feel guilty about food waste | Environmental guilt + daily habits = relatable tension. |

### How to Find Human Truths

1. **Ask uncomfortable questions**: Go beyond surface-level surveys.
2. **Listen for unexpected themes**: In qualitative research, the surprising responses are gold.
3. **Look for emotional contradictions**: Where do people say one thing but do another?
4. **Observe behavior, not just opinions**: What people do reveals more than what they say.
5. **Find the "hiding in plain sight" truth**: It should feel obvious once articulated, but nobody was saying it.
6. **Test with the "me too" reaction**: A good human truth makes people say "That's so me."

## 10. Modern Digital Hooks

### The 3-Second Rule

On social media, you have **3 seconds or less** to capture attention. The hook must work in this window or you've lost the scroll.

### What Stops the Scroll in 2026

**The "Ugly Ad" Phenomenon**: Lo-fi, handheld, barely-edited UGC-style creative now consistently outperforms high-production ads. Our brains have become expert ad-detectors, and the moment something looks "too produced," we scroll past it.

**Core principle**: The best-performing ads in 2026 don't look like ads. They look like content a friend sent you.

### Platform-Specific Findings

| Platform | Best Format | Key Insight |
|---|---|---|
| TikTok | Raw creator-led video | 77% more engagement than carousels. 80% of users seek entertainment, not ads. |
| Instagram | Reels for discovery, Carousels for conversion | Reels get 2.25x wider reach; carousels get 109% higher per-person engagement. |
| Facebook | "Ugly" lo-fi video | Engagement speed > polish. Authentic beats produced. |
| LinkedIn | Carousels with data | 7.00% engagement rate -- highest across all platforms studied. |
| YouTube | Shorts (TikTok rules) + Standard (5-sec hook) | 70B daily Short views. Standard ads must hook before the skip button. |

### Hook Mechanics That Work

1. **Start with the payoff, not the setup**: Lead with the most compelling moment.
2. **Curiosity gap**: Create a knowledge gap that demands closing. "I tried this for 30 days and..."
3. **Pattern interrupt**: Visual or tonal surprise in the first frame.
4. **Direct address**: "You" language creates immediate personal relevance.
5. **Contrarian statement**: Challenge a common belief. "Everything you know about X is wrong."
6. **Specific number**: "The 3 things that..." -- specificity signals value.
7. **Social proof**: "10M people have seen this..." -- herd curiosity.
8. **Sound-off design**: Anchor context with on-screen text. Most social video is watched muted.

### The Zeigarnik Effect in Digital

The Zeigarnik effect (people remember incomplete tasks better than completed ones) is the engine behind "open loops":

- **Tease and delay**: Reveal part of the answer, promise the rest.
- **Serial content**: Multi-part series that create anticipation.
- **Cliffhangers**: End before resolution to drive follow/subscribe behavior.

**Example**: Axe's 2007 campaign launched cryptic TV commercials that ended abruptly without revealing the product, driving viewers online to solve the mystery.

### Creator-Led > Brand-Led

"Your customers trust other customers way more than they trust beautifully polished brand ads." In 2026, the most effective paid social creative is:
- Shot on phones, not studios
- Featuring real people, not actors
- Narrated conversationally, not scripted
- Formatted natively for each platform

**Key stat**: 73% of consumers say they'd switch to a competitor if a brand feels inauthentic.

## 11. Codifiable Patterns & System Design

### Summary: Principles That Can Be Systematized

Based on all research, here are the actionable patterns that could be codified into a campaign generation system:

### A. The Insight-to-Idea Pipeline

```
1. HUMAN TRUTH    --> Find the universal emotional tension
2. BRAND TRUTH    --> Connect it to what the brand uniquely offers
3. BISOCIATION    --> Marry the human truth with an unexpected creative domain
4. TEMPLATE       --> Apply one of Goldenberg's 8 creative templates
5. STICKINESS     --> Validate against SUCCESs criteria
6. SIMPLIFICATION --> Compress to Commander's Intent (3-5 words)
```

### B. The Stickiness Checklist (Score Each 1-10)

For any campaign concept, score against:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| Simple | Can a 10-year-old understand the core message? |
| Unexpected | Does it violate a category norm or expectation? |
| Concrete | Can you picture it? Is it sensory and specific? |
| Credible | Would the audience believe this without further proof? |
| Emotional | Does it trigger a specific, nameable emotion? |
| Story | Is there a character, tension, and resolution? |
| Human Truth | Does it tap a universal, often-unspoken truth? |
| Distinctive | Would it stand out in a lineup of competitor ads? (Von Restorff) |
| Repeatable | Could this idea sustain a campaign across multiple executions? |

### C. The Eight Creative Templates (Goldenberg)

Apply these systematically to any product/insight combination:

1. **Unification**: What existing element in the audience's world could take on an additional role related to the product?
2. **Activation**: How could the audience participate or interact with the message?
3. **Metaphor**: What symbolic image perfectly captures the product benefit?
4. **Subtraction**: What happens if we remove the product from the scenario? What's the consequence?
5. **Extreme Consequence**: What's the most extreme (positive) result of using the product?
6. **Absurd Alternative**: What ridiculous thing would people do if this product didn't exist?
7. **Inversion**: What if we told the story from the opposite perspective?
8. **Extreme Effort**: What extreme lengths would someone go to for this product?

### D. Emotional Trigger Selection Matrix

| Emotion | Best For | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Humor | Shareability, recall, low-involvement products | Can overwhelm the message |
| Fear | Safety, health, insurance, prevention | Paralysis if no clear action given |
| Belonging | Community brands, lifestyle, identity | Can feel exclusionary |
| Aspiration | Premium brands, fitness, education | Can feel aspirational to the point of alienation |
| Nostalgia | Heritage brands, food, family products | Can feel backward-looking |
| Surprise/Awe | Launch campaigns, brand repositioning | Must be relevant, not random |
| Empathy/Compassion | Social causes, healthcare, non-profits | Must be authentic or backfires spectacularly |

### E. The Campaign Line Test

A great campaign line passes ALL of these:

1. **The Bar Test**: Would someone say this in a bar? (Natural language)
2. **The T-Shirt Test**: Would someone wear this on a T-shirt? (Identity-worthy)
3. **The Parody Test**: Could people make their own versions? (Cultural penetration)
4. **The 10-Year Test**: Will this still be relevant in a decade? (Timelessness)
5. **The Category Escape Test**: Does this transcend the product category? (Universal truth)
6. **The Opposite Test**: Is the opposite of this statement also interesting? If not, your claim is too generic.

### F. Digital Hook Framework

For social media creative specifically:

```
HOOK (0-3 sec):  Pattern interrupt + curiosity gap
HOLD (3-15 sec): Deliver on the hook's promise with concrete proof
PAYOFF (15+ sec): Emotional resolution + clear CTA
```

**Hook formulas that work**:
- "[Contrarian claim] -- here's why"
- "I [did X] for [time period] and [unexpected result]"
- "The [number] [things/reasons/ways] that [specific outcome]"
- "Stop [common behavior]. Do [this] instead."
- "What [authority figure] won't tell you about [topic]"
- "This is the [superlative] [thing] I've ever [verb]"
